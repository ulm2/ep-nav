🧭 Using a Compass

A compass always points North! The red end of the needle shows you where North is.

⚠️ IMPORTANT WARNING!

Keep metal objects away from your compass! Things like keys, phones, magnets, belt buckles, and metal jewelry can make the needle point the wrong way. Always check your compass is at least 2 feet away from metal objects!

Basic Directions:

  • North (N) - Top of most maps
  • East (E) - Right side (where the sun rises)
  • South (S) - Bottom of most maps
  • West (W) - Left side (where the sun sets)

📏 Click to learn: Understanding Scale

The scale tells you how distances on the map relate to real distances.

Example: If the scale says "1 inch = 1 mile", then 1 inch on your map equals 1 mile in real life!

Look for the scale bar on your map (usually at the bottom) to measure how far things really are.

🧭 Click to learn: Orienting Your Map

Step 1: Find the North arrow on your map (usually points up)

Step 2: Use your compass to find which way is North

Step 3: Turn your map so the North arrow points the same way as your compass needle

Now your map matches the real world around you!

📱 Using a Lensatic Compass with Maps & Apps

Use this section while a parent holds the phone or atlas. The parent reads from the map app or book, and you use the lensatic compass and paper map!

👨‍👩‍👧 Activity: Parent + Kid Bearing Team

Parent uses a map app, kid uses the lensatic compass.

Setup:

  1. Go to a park, playground, or open area.
  2. Parent opens a map app and chooses a nearby object (bench, tree, playground, statue).
  3. Parent taps the starting point (you) and the object to read the bearing (direction in degrees).

How to play:

  1. Parent says: "The bench is at 60° from where we are."
  2. Kid turns the lensatic compass bezel to 60°, lines up the needle with North, and faces that direction.
  3. Kid walks that direction while counting steps.
  4. When kid thinks they’re close, they look up and try to spot the object.
  5. Switch roles: Kid picks a new object on the map and gives the bearing to the parent!
💡 What you might discover: Phone compasses can be less accurate because of magnetic interference from the phone itself. Lensatic compasses are usually more reliable, especially when you keep them away from metal.
🎯 Activity: Bearing Relay Race

Team challenge: Parent gives bearings from the map app, kid navigates with the lensatic compass!

Setup:

  1. Go to a park, playground, or open area.
  2. Parent secretly hides 5 small objects (coins, toys, treats).
  3. Parent marks each hiding spot on their map app or on a printed map.
  4. Parent calculates the bearing to each spot from the starting point.

The Race:

  1. Parent says: "Object 1 is at bearing 80°, about 150 steps."
  2. Kid sets compass to 80° and walks that direction counting steps.
  3. When kid thinks they're close, they search for the object.
  4. When found, parent gives the next bearing!
  5. Race against the clock or try to find all objects using the fewest total steps.

Advanced version: Parent only gives bearings, no step counts. Kid has to use the map app or scale bar on a paper map to estimate distance before starting!

📖 Activity: Create Your Own Atlas Page

Make a detailed map of your neighborhood like a real atlas!

  1. Pick your area: Maybe the 4-block area around your home.
  2. Walk the area with a parent:
    • Use the lensatic compass to record the direction (bearing) of major streets.
    • Use a map app or measuring tape to get approximate distances.
    • Take photos of interesting landmarks.
  3. At home, create your map page:
    • Draw streets to scale (use map or app for measurements).
    • Add a compass rose showing North.
    • Mark important places: home, friends' houses, parks, stores.
    • Create a legend with symbols.
    • Add fun notes: "Best climbing tree at 45° from this corner."
  4. Test your atlas page: Can a friend or sibling use it to navigate to a specific place using just compass bearings from your map?
🎨 Make it special: Add color, drawings of buildings, and mark where you've seen interesting animals or plants. This becomes YOUR personal atlas page!
🌍 Activity: Google Earth + Compass Adventure

Plan a REAL adventure using satellite views and your lensatic compass!

Phase 1: Digital Planning

  1. With a parent, open Google Earth or satellite view in a map app.
  2. Find a local trail, park, or hiking area.
  3. Plan a route from Point A to Point B.
  4. Measure the bearing for each leg of the journey.
  5. Measure distances between turns.
  6. Print or sketch the route with bearings written down.

Phase 2: Real World Adventure

  1. Bring your notes, lensatic compass, paper map (if you have one), and this device.
  2. Start at Point A.
  3. Set your first bearing and walk it.
  4. At each waypoint, check your position on the map app with your parent.
  5. Record how accurate your compass navigation was!

Challenge mode: Can you complete the route using ONLY your compass and notes, only checking the app at the end?

❓ Activity: Lensatic Compass Question Corner

Kids often wonder what all the parts of a lensatic compass are for.

  • Sighting wire and lens: Help you line up a faraway object and read its exact bearing.
  • Bezel ring and clicks: Let you set a direction and keep it, even when you look away.
  • Luminous dots: Glow in the dark so you can still follow a bearing at night.
  • Index line: The line where you read the degrees you’re traveling.
  • Map use: Place the compass on a paper map to measure the bearing from your house to a park, check which way a trail is heading, or turn the map until the needle matches the map’s North arrow.

Parent tip: Let kids ask “Why does it do that?” and explore the answer together using the compass and map in front of you.

☀️ Using the Sun

The sun is a natural compass!

  • Morning: The sun rises in the East
  • Noon: The sun is in the South (in Northern Hemisphere)
  • Evening: The sun sets in the West

Remember: The sun moves from East to West across the sky during the day!

⭐ Finding North with Stars

The Big Dipper & Little Dipper

Big Dipper

The two yellow stars are the "pointer stars"

Little Dipper

Polaris (North Star) - the bright gold star!

How to Find Polaris (North Star):

  1. Find the Big Dipper in the night sky
  2. Look at the two stars at the end of the "cup" (the pointer stars)
  3. Draw an imaginary line through these two stars
  4. Follow that line about 5 times the distance between the pointer stars
  5. You'll find Polaris, the North Star! It's at the end of the Little Dipper's handle
  6. Polaris always points North!

🚶 Scale Challenge Activities

Let's test if your map scale matches real walking distances!

🎯 Activity 1: The 100-Step Challenge

What you'll need: Your map, ruler, compass, and this phone/tablet

  1. Mark your starting point on the map
  2. Pick a landmark you can see (tree, mailbox, bench)
  3. Measure the distance on your map using the scale
  4. Walk to it counting your steps
  5. Compare: Did the map distance match your walked distance?

Parent tip: Hide a small reward at the landmark before you start!

Track your results:
Map says: _____ feet/meters
You walked: _____ steps
Time it took: _____ minutes
🗺️ Activity 2: Three-Point Treasure Hunt

Create a mini adventure with 3 checkpoints!

  1. Parents: Before the walk, pick 3 locations and mark them on the map
  2. Hide small "treasures" or clues at each spot
  3. Kids: Use compass to navigate to each point
  4. At each checkpoint, record:
    • What the map distance said: _____
    • How many steps you took: _____
    • What direction you walked: _____
  5. After all 3 points, compare: Were your map measurements accurate?

Make it harder: Only use compass directions, not street names!

📱 Activity 3: Map vs App Challenge

Compare old-school navigation with modern tech!

Route 1 - Using Paper Map:

  • Plan a route on paper map from point A to B
  • Use compass to navigate
  • Count your steps and time yourself
  • Write down what you notice along the way

Route 2 - Using Phone Map:

  • Walk the same route using a map app
  • Record the app's distance measurement
  • Compare time and experience
Discussion questions:
• Which method felt more like an adventure?
• Which distances were more accurate?
• What would you do if your phone battery died?
• Did you notice different things with each method?
🎲 Activity 4: Mystery Distance Game

Can you guess distances without measuring first?

  1. Parent picks a visible landmark
  2. Kid guesses how far away it is: _____ feet/meters
  3. Measure it on the map: _____ feet/meters
  4. Walk to it counting steps: _____ steps
  5. Calculate actual distance using your step length

Bonus: After practicing, kids usually get really good at estimating distances!

Your step length:
Measure 10 steps, divide by 10 = _____ feet per step
(This helps convert steps to real distance!)

History/Culture: One of the biggest urban mountain parks in the U.S. — right inside El Paso.

🧩 Riddle:

I rise above the desert floor, with rocky trails and views galore. In Texas I stand tall and proud, above the city, near the clouds. What am I?

Answer: The Franklin Mountains!

🎁 Reward Joke:

Why don’t the Franklin Mountains ever get lost? Because they always peak in the right direction! 😄

History/Culture: A famous river that helped shape life, travel, and the borderlands.

🧩 Riddle:

I separate two lands but flow as one, I sparkle bright in desert sun. I’ve seen traders and explorers too, and I still keep flowing right past you. Who am I?

Answer: The Rio Grande!

🎁 Reward Joke:

Why did the river win the race? Because it had a current advantage! 🌊

Why don’t old missions gossip? Because they keep everything under adobe control! 😄
